Cyber Insurance for Technology Companies

Data breach response, ransomware, business interruption, regulatory defense, and social engineering coverage. Placed by a broker who understands both the technology and the policy language.

What cyber insurance covers

A cyber liability policy responds when your company experiences a security incident, data breach, or technology failure that causes financial harm. For technology companies, the key coverage areas are:

  • Data breach response and notification. Forensic investigation, legal counsel, notification to affected individuals, credit monitoring, and public relations costs.
  • Ransomware and cyber extortion. Ransom payments (where legal), negotiation costs, and business interruption during the event.
  • Business interruption. Lost income and extra expenses when your systems go down, whether from an attack, a system failure, or a cloud provider outage.
  • Regulatory defense and penalties. Legal costs to defend against regulatory investigations and, where insurable, fines and penalties.
  • Social engineering and funds transfer fraud. Coverage when an employee is tricked into sending money to a fraudulent account.
  • Third-party liability. Claims from clients or partners who suffer losses because of a breach at your company.

What we look for that other brokers miss

Not all cyber policies are the same. The differences are in the definitions, exclusions, and sublimits that most brokers do not read. Here is what we check:

  • Does "computer system" include cloud infrastructure? If your operations run on AWS, Azure, or GCP, and the policy defines "computer system" as hardware you own or operate, a cloud outage may not be covered.
  • Are sublimits adequate? A $2 million cyber policy with a $100,000 sublimit on business interruption provides $100,000 of business interruption coverage, not $2 million. Many policies bury sublimits in the endorsements.
  • Does ransomware coverage survive sanctions screening? OFAC sanctions compliance provisions can void ransomware payments. Some policies handle this better than others.
  • Is social engineering included or excluded? Some policies include it. Some exclude it. Some cover it with a sublimit so low it is meaningless. The application may not even ask about it.
  • What are the security warranty requirements? Some policies require specific security controls (MFA, endpoint detection, encrypted backups). If you do not meet those requirements at the time of a claim, the carrier can deny it.

Common questions about cyber insurance

Does my cyber policy cover ransomware payments?

Most cyber policies include ransomware coverage, but the details vary significantly. Some policies require prior written consent from the carrier before payment. Some apply sublimits. Some include OFAC sanctions exclusions that can void coverage entirely. The answer depends on your specific policy language, not the marketing materials.

Do I need cyber insurance if I already have Tech E&O?

Yes. Tech E&O covers claims from clients when your product or service fails. Cyber insurance covers your own losses from security incidents. They address different exposures. A client suing you because your software had a vulnerability is a Tech E&O claim. Your company losing revenue because your systems were encrypted by ransomware is a cyber claim.

How much cyber insurance does a technology company need?

It depends on your revenue, the data you handle, your client contracts, and your regulatory environment. Client contracts often specify minimum limits ($1M, $3M, $5M). Start there and adjust based on your actual exposure. A broker who understands technology companies can help you right-size the coverage without overpaying.
