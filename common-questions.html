Common Questions

Getting started

What insurance does my professional services firm need?

At minimum, most professional services firms need general liability, professional liability (E&O), and cyber insurance. Beyond that, D&O, workers comp, EPLI, and umbrella coverage depend on your firm's size, structure, and what your contracts require. Tell me about your practice and I'll tell you what you need and what you don't.

Does my accounting firm or law firm need cyber insurance?

Yes. Accounting firms hold client tax returns, financial statements, and Social Security numbers. Law firms hold privileged communications and confidential case files. Both are high-value targets. A data breach at a professional services firm affects every client whose data you hold. Cyber insurance covers breach response costs, regulatory defense, business interruption, and client notification. Most professional liability policies exclude cyber claims, so this is separate coverage.

I'm starting a firm. Where do I even begin?

Start with what's required. Your lease probably requires general liability. Your state probably requires workers comp if you have employees. Many client contracts specify insurance minimums, especially professional liability. I can walk you through the whole thing and set up a program that covers what matters without overbuying.

How much should I be paying?

It varies by profession, revenue, headcount, and claims history. A 5-person accounting firm pays differently than a 5-person law firm. I can benchmark your premiums against similar firms and tell you if you're overpaying, underpaying, or in the right range. More importantly, I'll tell you if you're paying for the right things.

After you're covered

My client needs a certificate of insurance. How do I get one?

I handle that. Tell me what the client needs (usually it's on the contract) and I'll get the certificate issued. If your current coverage doesn't meet their requirements, I'll tell you what needs to change and what it costs before we make any changes.

What's a retroactive date and why does it matter?

Your professional liability policy has a retroactive date. It's the earliest date for which claims can be covered. If someone files a claim about work you did before that date, your policy won't cover it. This matters when switching carriers or buying a new policy. I make sure the retroactive date covers your full history of professional work, not just the current policy period.

My client contract requires specific coverage limits. How do I check?

Send me the contract requirements and your current policy declarations page. I'll compare them and tell you exactly where you stand: what meets the requirement, what doesn't, and what it costs to close the gap. This is one of the most common requests I handle.

What happens when I file a claim?

You report the claim to your carrier -- I can help you do this and make sure it's reported correctly. They assign an adjuster who investigates. Then they pay, deny, or negotiate. If something doesn't look right, I'll push back. But the best claim strategy is knowing what your policy says before you need to use it.

My renewal came in higher. Is that normal?

Rates go up for lots of reasons -- market conditions, claims history, industry trends. But "the market went up" isn't always the real answer. Sometimes it's worth shopping. Sometimes the increase is justified but you can restructure to reduce cost. I'll look at the renewal, tell you why it went up, and whether it makes sense to push back or move.

Can I switch brokers in the middle of a policy?

Yes. Your policy stays the same -- you're just changing who services it. You can switch brokers at any time. Most people do it at renewal, but you don't have to wait. If you're not getting the attention you need, reach out and we can talk about it.
