Technology E&O Insurance

Professional liability for when your technology product or service causes a client financial harm. I make sure the policy covers what you actually do, not what the application assumed.

What it covers

  • Software defects or failures. Your code has a bug that causes a client's system to go down or lose data.
  • Service delivery failures. Missed deadlines, misconfigured systems, or work that doesn't meet contracted specs.
  • Negligent advice. A client follows your technical recommendation and it causes them financial harm.
  • Intellectual property claims. Allegations that your product infringes on a patent, copyright, or trade secret.
  • Data loss or corruption. Your service causes a client to lose or corrupt their data.

The gap most brokers miss

Every Tech E&O policy defines "professional services" or "technology services." That definition controls what is covered. If it doesn't match what your company actually does, the policy doesn't respond when you need it.

Most brokers don't check. They submit the application, get a quote, and never read the definition. I do.

How this goes wrong:

  • IT firm that does security monitoring. Policy defines services as "IT consulting and support." Carrier argues security monitoring isn't consulting. Claim denied.
  • Software company that recommends third-party tools. Policy excludes liability from "products not developed by the insured." Not covered.
  • MSP that does penetration testing. Policy has an intentional acts exclusion. Carrier argues pen testing is intentional unauthorized access. Disputed.

Tech E&O vs. General E&O

General professional liability is designed for traditional professions: accountants, consultants, architects. Tech E&O covers technology-specific exposures like software failures, system downtime, and data loss. If you're a tech company with a general E&O policy, you likely have gaps.
