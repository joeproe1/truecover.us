Directors & Officers Insurance

Management liability for founders, board members, and company officers. Most institutional investors require D&O within 60-90 days of Series A close. We help you get the right coverage from the start.

What D&O insurance covers

Directors and Officers (D&O) insurance protects the personal assets of company leaders and the company itself when management decisions lead to claims. For technology companies, D&O responds to:

  • Investor claims. Allegations of misrepresentation, breach of fiduciary duty, or mismanagement of company funds.
  • Regulatory investigations. Defense costs when a regulator investigates the company's business practices, data handling, or compliance.
  • Employment claims. Wrongful termination, discrimination, or harassment allegations (often covered under EPLI, but some D&O policies include employment-related claims).
  • Customer or competitor claims. Allegations of unfair business practices, anti-competitive behavior, or misleading statements.
  • Securities claims. For companies that issue equity, claims related to the offering, valuation statements, or shareholder disputes.

D&O after fundraising

Most venture capital term sheets require D&O insurance with minimum limits of $3-5 million. The requirement typically takes effect at close, giving the company 60-90 days to bind coverage. This is not optional. Investors require it because they are joining your board and need personal protection.

If you are raising a Series A or B, getting D&O in place should be on your closing checklist alongside legal and banking.

What to watch for

  • Entity coverage vs. individual-only. Some D&O policies only cover individual directors and officers. Others cover the company entity as well. For private companies, entity coverage matters because the company is usually the target of claims, not individual directors.
  • Regulatory investigation coverage. Not all D&O policies cover regulatory investigations. For technology companies in regulated industries (FinTech, health tech, EdTech), this is critical.
  • Prior acts coverage. If you are buying D&O for the first time, prior acts coverage extends protection to decisions made before the policy inception date. Without it, any claim arising from pre-policy decisions is excluded.
  • Insured vs. insured exclusion. This exclusion prevents one insured person from suing another under the policy. For startups, this can matter in co-founder disputes. Some policies carve this back for derivative actions or employment claims.

Common questions about D&O insurance

When do I need D&O insurance?

If you have outside investors, a board of directors, or significant revenue, you should have D&O. Most companies buy it at Series A when investors require it. But even bootstrapped companies with employees and customers face management liability risk.

How much D&O coverage does a startup need?

Investor term sheets typically require $3-5 million in limits. The right amount depends on your company's stage, industry, regulatory exposure, and the specifics of your cap table. A broker who works with technology companies can help you benchmark against similar companies.

What is the difference between D&O and EPLI?

D&O covers claims against management for decisions they made in their capacity as company leaders. EPLI (Employment Practices Liability Insurance) specifically covers employment-related claims like wrongful termination, discrimination, and harassment. There is some overlap, and some policies bundle them. Whether to buy them separately or combined depends on your specific situation.
